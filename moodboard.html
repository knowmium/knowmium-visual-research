03 — Principles

Design Principles

Six rules that govern every visual decision in Fernwae.

Warm, Not Cold

Data visualization doesn't have to feel clinical. Cream backgrounds, serif type, hand-drawn quality. Every screen should feel like opening a beautiful book.

Precision, Not Clutter

Tufte's principle: maximize the data-ink ratio. Every pixel earns its place. White space is as important as the data it frames.

Scientific, Not Corporate

The aesthetic of 18th-century astronomy plates, not SaaS dashboards. Engraved lines, not gradient fills. Annotations, not tooltips.

Human, Not Machine

Organic forms over rigid grids. Conversation flows like rivers, not spreadsheets. The visualization should feel alive.

Layered, Not Flat

Surface text, emotional undercurrent, cognitive subtext — all visible simultaneously. Like geological strata or a topographic map.

Editorial, Not Utilitarian

Every screen is a page in a magazine. Typography matters. Spacing matters. The experience of reading the data is as important as the data itself.

The Extended Canon

Beyond Felton and Lupi — the full constellation of creators who treat data with the warmth, craft, and editorial care that defines the Fernwae register.

Federica Fragapane — Animal Kingdom

Organic, nature-inspired data forms. Hand-illustrated species rendered as data points. Fragapane treats every visualization like a botanical plate — warm colors, delicate linework, and a sense of wonder. Her work for National Geographic and La Lettura is the gold standard for editorial data art.

W.E.B. Du Bois — 1900 Paris Exposition

Hand-drawn data portraits from 1900. Bold geometric forms, warm earth tones, spiral charts that feel alive. Du Bois proved 125 years ago that data visualization could be both rigorous and deeply human. The original editorial data artist.

Smithsonian feature → Pioneer

RJ Andrews — Profiling the Parks

Vintage-style data illustration. 3D terrain profiles rendered from USGS data with the warmth of a 19th-century expedition journal. His book Info We Trust is the definitive history of making data beautiful. Exactly the Fernwae register.

Info We Trust → Essential

Mona Chalabi — Hand-Drawn Data

Data journalism drawn entirely by hand. Imperfect lines, warm humor, human touch in every chart. Chalabi's Guardian column proved that hand-drawn data is more trustworthy and engaging than polished machine-made charts. The antidote to corporate dashboards.

View work → Hand-Drawn

Sonja Kuijpers — Seasonal Calendar

Circular data visualization of the Japanese seasonal calendar. Intricate, meditative, printed as a physical book. Data treated as craft object. The level of care and intentionality here is what Fernwae aspires to.

Stefanie Posavec's Writing Without Words is the single most directly relevant precedent for Fernwae. She proved that the structure of language — sentence length, rhythm, word choice — can be rendered as organic, beautiful visual form.

Posavec — Writing Without Words

Sentence structure as branching organic form. Color-coded by theme, sized by length. This is the closest existing work to what Fernwae is building — but applied to books rather than live conversation.
